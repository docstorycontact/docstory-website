Student Interview Allopathic (MD)

Dominic Carusillo, MS1 — USC Keck School of Medicine

DC
Dominic Carusillo
MS1 at USC Keck School of Medicine · May 2018

What does your typical day of medical school look like?

Dominic describes waking at 6:30am, attending the gym until 9:30am, studying from 10am-12pm, attending afternoon sessions or studying, finishing around 5pm, then studying again from 8pm-10pm before relaxing until 11:30pm.

How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?

"You can't blow off class as much as you could in undergrad." Medical school covers significantly more material weekly. He emphasizes learning concepts at a "superficial level" rather than requiring deep understanding like undergraduate demanded.

How has your approach to learning/studying changed since undergrad?

Dominic became more consistent, treating school as a full-time job. He prioritizes efficiency and relies heavily on third-party resources like Pathoma videos over lectures.

What are the most important things to learn about a medical school when deciding fit?

Webcasted lectures are essential for saving time. Hospital proximity matters for research and shadowing opportunities. Most schools are similar except top research institutions.

Can you describe the area surrounding your school?

The immediate campus area is economically impoverished. He appreciates LA's activities (shows, hikes, bars, beach) but dislikes traffic and air quality.

What organizations are you involved in?

Dominic participates in several student interest groups. He mentions the HTE program (Health, Technology and Engineering) as potentially unique to USC.

In retrospect, which classes in undergrad were most useful?

Biochemistry proved useful; an upper-division cancer course provided foundational understanding; undergraduate research helped with scientific paper reading and familiarity with research concepts.

What is a unique aspect of education at your school?

Shadowing opportunities stand out as most valuable, allowing exposure to specialties and direct mentorship from attending physicians.

What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?

He gained better insight into how different specialties can be "even though they are both 'doctors'."

If a young undergraduate came to you asking about the best major, what would you say?

Choose a major you enjoy while satisfying med school prerequisites. Balance interests with GPA competitiveness — avoid both "easy" majors and challenging ones that sabotage grades.

What do you wish you had known as an undergraduate?

Letters of recommendation require early planning. He suggests contacting professors in fall before June applications to avoid secondary deadlines stress.

What is one thing you would do differently?

Dominic would have studied more in his first two years and engaged more with faculty despite feeling anxious. He acknowledges imposter syndrome affects many competent people.
