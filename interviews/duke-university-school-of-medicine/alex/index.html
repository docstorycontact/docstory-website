Student Interview Allopathic (MD)

Alex, MS2 — Duke University School of Medicine

AL
Alex
MS2 at Duke University School of Medicine · June 2018

What does your typical day of medical school look like?

"Throughout the first year, my schedule has varied depending on what class we are currently enrolled in." Classes range from lecture-heavy first semester to "flipped classroom" models. Lectures aren't mandatory and are recorded. Classes typically meet 3-4 days weekly from 9-10 AM with lunch from 12-1 PM. "I do most of my studying in the afternoon/evening" and sets aside about an hour daily for exercise, describing having "a really great work-life balance."

How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?

Alex notes being an English major with different background than typical applicants. At the post-baccalaureate program and large state school, professors taught hundreds of students. "However, at Duke, I feel like the environment here is that everyone wants you to succeed." The cohort of ~115 students gets to know professors well, and anyone at the medical center is "just an email away."

What have you liked about the teaching practices at your institution?

"I really like that Duke keeps our mandatory class time to a minimum and tries to maximize our independent study time."

Looking back on when you were applying to medical schools, what do you think are the most important things to learn about a medical school?

"The most important thing was ultimately the happiness of the student body." Alex sought a condensed pre-clinical curriculum and "felt like the Duke students were some of the happiest students I met on the interview trail."

Can you give us a brief description of the area surrounding your school?

Durham has about 250,000 people, located 15 minutes from Chapel Hill and 25 minutes from Raleigh. "From my house, I can run to multiple miles of wooded trails." The Duke Forest offers exploration opportunities. Downtown is 3 miles from campus with "insanely cheap" cost of living compared to East Coast cities.

What organizations or activities are you involved in outside of your regular classwork?

Alex participates in curriculum reform groups and Duke Med Elementary, teaching Durham third-graders about organ systems weekly. Classmates started "Duke Med for Social Justice" addressing healthcare inequities. "If you're passionate about something, and want to create a student group, go for it."

How much/well have you been able to develop clinical skills alongside your classroom work?

First-year students have "Clinical Skills Foundation," a weekly 3-hour class covering physical exams, differentials, and patient interviewing. Second year begins with a 3-week "Clinical Skills Intensive" crash course for ward work, followed by weekly ongoing sessions.

What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?

"You have to accept that it's impossible to know everything, and that medicine is a career that requires you to be a lifelong learner!"

Are there any resources that stand out as most useful for self-learning?

"Pathoma + SketchyMedical have been really helpful to consolidate information." Alex is "a huge believer in Anki and pre-made Anki decks."

If a young undergraduate interested in applying to medical school came to you seeking advice about which major is best, what would you tell them?

"Dude. I was an English Literature major. You can major in whatever you're interested in, as long as you get the necessary pre-reqs (or do a post-bacc)!"

What is your favorite event of the year put on by your medical school?

Favorite events are often impromptu class GroupMe gatherings, like a spontaneous swimming quarry trip with 20 classmates. Official Duke-sanctioned fall medical school dance features "an open bar" with guaranteed nights off.

What do you wish you had known as an undergraduate/in the application process?

"Gap years are okay! Though I didn't have to, reapplying and working on fine-tuning your application is also okay! Be unique and pursue what interests you."
