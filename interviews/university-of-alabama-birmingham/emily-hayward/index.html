Student Interview Allopathic (MD)

Emily Hayward, MS3 (MD/PhD) — University of Alabama at Birmingham (MSTP)

EH
Emily Hayward
MS3 (MD/PhD) at University of Alabama at Birmingham (MSTP) · November 2018

What does your typical day look like? How do your classes compare to undergraduate?

Emily has written a detailed piece on medical school class structure on her blog UnABridged, which provides insight into her first-year MD/PhD schedule. Rather than pursuing perfect test scores, she now focuses on becoming a better physician and connecting material to future patient care.

She advises: "Medical school is all about taking advantage of learning opportunities congruent with the physician you wish to become." Beyond memorizing biochemical pathways, students should seek mentors, volunteer with relevant organizations, spend time with patients and families affected by illness, and engage meaningfully with patients during interviews.

Can you describe the area surrounding your school and what you do for fun?

Emily greatly enjoys living in Birmingham. As an introvert preferring quiet, safe spaces without heavy traffic or high costs, Birmingham proved ideal. The city provides academic resources while maintaining affordability and allowing reasonable commutes.

NIH-funded MD/PhD students receive monthly stipends that stretch further in Birmingham. Many peers purchased homes within 10-15 minutes of campus. Emily rents a 1,200-square-foot house with a fenced yard five minutes from campus.

Drawbacks for some peers include limited entertainment events and concerts, plus scarce downtown housing near campus.

What resources have been most useful for self-learning?

Emily strongly advises against starting boards resources before medical school begins — burnout risk is substantial. She is an auditory learner who first watches someone explain material, then uses condensed notes and flashcards for detailed recall.

Sketchy Micro: Brief, dense videos (3-4 minutes) with cartoon illustrations representing pathogens. Color-coding and symbolic imagery (purple for gram-positive, red for gram-negative) made this superior to rote memorization.

Pathoma: Video series with bullet-point book ($120 for 21 months). Dr. Husein Sattar condenses pathology into 35 hours, emphasizing underlying principles. Emily used it before lectures for background or after for exam review.

Boards and Beyond: Dr. Jason Ryan's concise videos ($150/year or $250/two years). Over 120 hours total but 4-12 hours per module, allowing incorporation during coursework rather than cramming before Step 1. She considered it "the Pathoma of everything else."

Supplementary: BRS Physiology for text-based learning, YouTube for real patient stories (especially genetic/metabolic diseases).

How have you developed clinical skills alongside classroom work?

Introduction to Clinical Medicine (ICM): UAB divides ~180 students into groups of six with a physician preceptor. Students learn physical examinations aligned with each organ module, observe preceptors, conduct their own examinations, learn medical record-keeping, and practice presenting cases.

Equal Access Birmingham (EAB): A student-run free clinic. Within her first two weeks, Emily obtained vital signs, interviewed patients, synthesized information, and proposed diagnoses and treatment plans under physician oversight.

What is one thing you would do differently in undergrad?

Avoid taking extremely challenging classes during senior year interview season. Emily juggled her hardest course load with extensive medical school applications and interviews, at one point working until 3am after a three-hour flight delay before waking at 5:30am for interviews.

How has self-doubt affected you and what helped you persevere?

Emily's MCAT score was notably weaker than her GPA — particularly low for fully-funded MD/PhD positions. Despite applying only to NIH-funded MSTP programs, she received multiple waitlists and waited a full year for acceptance to UAB.

Within a day of accepting, doubts emerged. She questioned whether she was truly intelligent enough and whether colleagues would discover her inadequacy.

Turning points: Honest conversations with peers revealed they shared identical doubts despite their evident brilliance. Strong mentors (Dr. Lorenz, Randy, and Dr. Van Wagoner) believed in her when she doubted herself. Rather than theorizing future problems, Emily concentrated on gratitude and created weekly goal lists, physically checking off completed tasks to visualize advancement.

How do you maintain your mental health?

Compartmentalization: Separate school from personal life when possible. Small study breaks and larger blocks of guilt-free personal time are essential.

Hobby maintenance: Emily uses writing as a core identity component and personal release, reflecting on her day, processing experiences, and developing her physician identity. Her writing created a unique niche distinguishing her from peers.

Mental health awareness: Emily references a JAMA meta-analysis: 27.2% of medical students experience depression or depressive symptoms, 11.1% have experienced suicidal ideation. "Taking care of yourself allows you to be at your best for those around you, for those you are trying to help." Seeking help demonstrates strength, not weakness.
