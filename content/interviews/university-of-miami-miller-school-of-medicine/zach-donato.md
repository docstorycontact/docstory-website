# Zach Donato, MS2 — University of Miami Miller School of Medicine

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

He wakes around 6 AM for hospital rotations, finishing between 2–6 PM depending on the rotation. He attends integrated didactic days that vary by rotation with "cut-out time in our schedule for that day to attend lectures."

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

Miami uses a case-based learning curriculum. During preclinical year, students "first learn the normal physiology of systems" then progress "block-by-block in a symptoms-based fashion." Unlike undergraduate "lectured at" experiences, medical school requires "much more responsibility on us to learn material on our own."

**Q: How has your approach to studying changed since undergrad?**

His studying became "much more structured," allocating time for each task. Rather than making study guides, he now uses "Anki flashcards for longitudinal learning, videos to enforce information, and UWorld to test my knowledge."

**Q: How would you describe the student culture at your school?**

Miami emphasizes service orientation. The DOCS clinic involves "over 70% of students participating" in student-run health fairs in underserved areas.

**Q: Can you describe Miami and what you do for fun?**

Most students live in Brickell or Downtown Miami. "There is a lot of fun night life" and students adopt a "work-hard-play-hard mentality," though living costs are expensive.

**Q: What would you consider strengths of your medical school?**

"Diverse patient and student population, very early clinical experience integrated into the curriculum, ample time for electives" and "non-cutthroat culture."

**Q: What resources have been most useful to you?**

Pathoma, Anki, Boards and Beyond, Amboss, and UWorld were most helpful. He characterizes Osmosis as "useless compared to these other amazing resources."

**Q: How have you been able to develop clinical skills?**

"Early clerkships has made the integration between the book knowledge and real-life knowledge much easier for me."

**Q: What is one thing you would do differently?**

"Organization, organization and…I'll say it again…organization."

**Q: How has self-doubt affected you?**

"White coat syndrome" is real. Confidence came through "personal growth in taking histories, physical exam maneuvers, and clinical knowledge" rather than comparing himself to others.

**Q: How do you maintain your mental health?**

"Do not make medical school your work and hobby." He maintains balance through running, reading, and scuba diving, emphasizing "If you don't have your mental health you will not learn anything well."
