# Chuck Chan, OSM1 — Western University of Health Sciences College of Osteopathic Medicine

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

"Typically we have 4-8 hours of class per day depending on the day of the week. 4 hours a week are spent learning to prepare for patient encounters and another 4 hours a week are spent on learning principles of OMM."

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

The material isn't harder but the pace is significantly faster. Multiple professors teach specialty topics within single courses using integrated curriculum. There are many lecture hours and people typically use webcasts to save time, with emphasis on clinically relevant and board-focused material.

**Q: How has your approach to learning/studying changed since undergrad?**

He switched to "getting information that is high-yield only." Chan uses Pathoma, Sketchy, and Boards and Beyond supplements plus Anki flashcards, contrasting with his undergrad approach of studying everything in depth.

**Q: What are the most important things to learn about a medical school when deciding fit?**

"The most important things to me would be putting myself in a situation where I could succeed. That includes having established curriculum, P/NP courses, going to a school with a good match history, location, presence of a teaching hospital, board scores, and research opportunities."

**Q: Can you describe the area surrounding your school?**

Pomona has medium crime levels (which he hasn't personally experienced). Downsides include limited food options compared to nearby Orange County or LA, though those areas are accessible after exams. Homelessness and poverty are prevalent, with limited activities in the city itself. While he feels relatively safe driving, he acknowledges nighttime walking isn't safe — and he volunteers at clinics to give back.

**Q: What organizations are you involved in?**

Chan participates in a Lifestyle Medicine track covering diet, physical health, and mental health. He notes osteopathic-specific clubs are prevalent, offering workshops on using OMM (Osteopathic Manipulative Medicine) to treat conditions like back and neck pain.

**Q: In retrospect, which classes in undergrad were most useful?**

"Physiology and biochemistry were probably the two most helpful classes for the MCAT and medical school." Molecular biology and endocrinology also helped. He found his undergrad anatomy class "useless" compared to medical school anatomy.

**Q: What is a unique aspect of education at your school?**

Eight patient encounters per semester provide excellent practice. A particularly valuable experience was a psychiatry-focused OSCE where students diagnosed various mental health conditions, allowing them to practice empathy and questioning techniques.

**Q: What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?**

Chan expresses ambivalence — some days discouraging him from recommending medical school, other days feeling blessed. His main challenge is the demanding schedule with few breaks, causing concern about long-term life balance regarding family and relationships.

**Q: If a young undergraduate came to you asking about the best major, what would you say?**

"I would tell them to pick whatever major they like the most, but to take science courses as electives to fill their schedule." He encourages undergraduates to explore subjects they won't study again rather than reinforce medical school material early.

**Q: What do you wish you had known as an undergraduate?**

"In general I think it is good to take advice with a grain of salt." Chan reflects that he sought advice too frequently without considering his own needs, suggesting self-awareness about personal strengths would have improved his success.

**Q: What is one thing you would do differently?**

He suggests skipping pre-med coursework to fully enjoy college, then completing a rigorous post-baccalaureate program — though acknowledging this approach is expensive.
