# Nick Bacher, MS3 — New York Medical College

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

First year involved structured labs and lectures with evening study time. Second year shifted to "more self-study: get up, study, lunch, study, dinner, relax, sleep" with labs 2–3 days weekly. Dedicated study was "just get up, study, sleep, repeat." Nick balances coursework with extracurriculars and personal relaxation, noting that "It's possible to have a life outside of schoolwork."

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

Medical school classmates are "hardworking brilliant students" who collaborate rather than compete. Nick initially feared medical school would be impossible, but realized "it's just a test, this is just class, and it's not that different from any science class I've taken before."

**Q: What are the most important things to learn about a medical school when deciding fit?**

Recognize that choosing between acceptances is "a great privilege." Prioritize community feel: "Your class will become your second family." Consider lecture requirements, clinical rotation locations, campus food, and student housing. And for NYMC specifically: just because they don't have pass/fail preclinical grading "doesn't mean we are competitive."

**Q: Can you describe the area surrounding your school?**

The suburban Westchester County location is "not walkable, but a close drive to several towns and just over half an hour by train to Manhattan." Nick appreciates accessing "the world's best everything" in nearby Manhattan.

**Q: What organizations are you involved in?**

Student government as a class representative and leading hands-free CPR initiatives as part of a national NYC program.

**Q: How have you been able to develop clinical skills?**

"Exceptionally good Standardized Patients" throughout first and second year provided valuable feedback. Second-year emergency department preceptorships were particularly beneficial for "seeing so much in a single four hour shift."

**Q: What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?**

He realized "there is so much more to medicine than I thought before" and discovered expanded career opportunities despite entering with specific specialty plans.

**Q: What resources have been most useful to you?**

"Boards and Beyond for during the year, Pathoma and UWorld for dedicated, First Aid for both." Wikipedia receives heavy daily use. "Group studying was the best way for me to stay engaged and really learn."

**Q: If a young undergraduate came to you asking about the best major, what would you say?**

Choose a major that is interesting, useful, and allows for strong grades. "The GPA from a Biology major and the GPA from an Engineering major are considered essentially the same. There's no extra points for picking something hard."

**Q: What is your favorite school event?**

The newly-launched Friendsgiving features "10–15 small group potluck dinners including students from all four years" plus appreciation ceremonies. It exemplifies the "tight knit community" at NYMC.

**Q: What do you wish you had known as an undergraduate?**

He wished he had known about community college credit challenges, grade importance, and application complexity earlier. "Start early, work hard in your classes, go to a four year school, and seek advising from more than just your school's pre-health advisors."
