# Jonathan Carnino, MS1

*Note: School not specified in interview. Interview focused on undergraduate and medical school research strategies.*

**Q: Did you focus on research during your undergraduate studies?**

Carnino began research the summer before junior year by emailing 5–10 PIs from Boston University's posting website. He secured a basic science lab position with summer funding, which led to part-time work junior year and full-time during the following summer. He remained part-time senior year. Through longitudinal involvement, he mastered techniques including RT-PCR, animal handling, Western Blot, and cell culture, eventually publishing his own projects.

**Q: What makes a great research mentor or PI?**

"A great PI is one who is willing to give a student responsibility and who publishes very often." He recommends checking yearly publication counts, whether undergrads appear on papers, and whether the PI has NIH funding. He cautions against PIs who assign repetitive single tasks.

**Q: How do you build rapport with a PI?**

"Never say no. Always take on any work your PI offers you." Willingness to learn new techniques and accepting challenges builds strong relationships.

**Q: How important was research for medical school admission?**

Research was a strong application component but surprisingly didn't arise frequently in interviews, which focused more on clinical experience. He recommends research involvement, particularly for competitive specialties.

**Q: When should you get involved in research in medical school?**

He got involved approximately halfway through undergrad, considering this optimal timing or potentially later. Grades take priority initially; research should supplement strong academics.

**Q: What types of research suit medical students?**

"Clinical research is the best area to get involved in" due to flexibility and reduced time commitment compared to basic research requiring months-long weekly commitments.

**Q: How should uncertain students approach research?**

Pursue exciting areas and seek PIs allowing independent projects. Being first-author carries more weight than assisting on smaller tasks.

**Q: If you can't publish, how do you derive value from research?**

First projects typically don't yield publications but provide learning experiences applied to future work.

**Q: How do you balance classes and research?**

"I live off my Google calendar" by scheduling all commitments weekly and following the plan consistently.
