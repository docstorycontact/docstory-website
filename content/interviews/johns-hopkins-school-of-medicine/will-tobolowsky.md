# Will Tobolowsky, MS3 — Johns Hopkins University School of Medicine

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

Pre-clinical years begin with 8AM lectures, typically 3-6 daily with small group and pathology sessions. Mondays/Wednesdays end around 3PM; Tuesdays/Thursdays around 12PM, with clinic work until 5PM on either Tuesday or Thursday. Fridays end around 1-2PM. Clinical years vary by rotation, starting with morning rounds, then patient care/OR/documentation.

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

"They are very similar. The biggest difference is that class is focused on the same field vs. in undergraduate where you might go from math to philosophy to a biology class." You get 4-8 hours of dedicated learning in a single subject. Classes run 2-4 weeks with 40-60 lectures versus college's 30 sessions spread across 10 weeks.

**Q: How has your approach to learning and/or studying changed since you were an undergrad?**

"Not much, I took written notes in undergrad and continued to do so in medical school." Daily review of lectures and weekend review of study materials for USMLE Step 1 are recommended. "Stick with whatever works for you as long as it isn't an unsustainable or unhealthy method."

**Q: How would you describe the unique culture at your school?**

The school hosts numerous events (Olympics, networking, social activities) with more opportunities than time available. Recommendation: "find specific events or groups that you like and stick with them" while balancing studies, rotations, and hobbies.

**Q: Can you give us a brief description of the area surrounding your school?**

Located in East Baltimore. Negatives include crime and safety concerns, limited food options without a car. Positives include excellent dining and nightlife with transportation. Important perspective: "I wouldn't let an area influence your choice of medical school too much."

**Q: What resources have been most useful to you?**

Sketchy Medical for microbiology and pharmacology; Pathoma for pathology (watch twice); First-Aid for USMLE Step 1 as a checklist and annotated reference; USMLE Rx for board-style questions during courses; U-World as the best question bank, recommended for second year and dedicated study.

**Q: What seemed to be important topics during your interviews?**

"I don't really remember too much; my advice is just to be yourself (unless you're not a very good person). Try to let your personality and passion for whatever may interest you come through."

**Q: What is one thing you would do differently in undergraduate or pre-med years?**

"Not much. My experience was what it was (good and bad), and I am grateful for that."

**Q: How has doubt affected you, and what helps you persevere?**

"Usually I try to re-direct my doubt as motivation to push myself to learn more and help my patients more compassionately."

**Q: How do you maintain mental health?**

Set aside time for family and friends. Reach out if experiencing doubt or depression. "Medical school is challenging, and no one is going to judge you for talking to a therapist."
