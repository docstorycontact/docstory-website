# Austin Wininger, MS3 — Indiana University School of Medicine

**Q: What was the most difficult adjustment going from second to third year?**

The transition was "the most difficult educational barrier I have faced." The shift to significantly increased self-directed learning — without clear resource guidance — was jarring. NBME exam results early on revealed insufficient preparation. He also emphasized the challenge of subjective clinical evaluations and the necessity of seeking faculty feedback, requiring him to overcome his natural reticence from earlier years.

**Q: Can you describe the basic structure of rotations at your institution?**

IUSM features eight core third-year clerkships: Surgery, Internal Medicine, OB/GYN, Psychiatry, Family Medicine, Anesthesia, Pediatrics, and Neurology. Surgery, Pediatrics, and IM are two months each. Most rotations include NBME exams except Family Medicine and Anesthesia. Grading uses P, HP, H scale with clinical evaluations and NBME exams comprising over 70% of grades.

**Q: What resources helped you through clerkship exams?**

He primarily utilized Online Med Ed and UWorld. He praised OME for "hitting the highlights for each specialty" and systematically completed all UWorld questions before NBME exams. He supplemented with First Aid, BRS, and Step Up to Medicine.

**Q: How would you describe your role on the team?**

Role varies significantly by specialty and location. Generally, students "function at an intern level," seeing patients independently then presenting to residents or attendings to develop interviewing, physical examination, and differential diagnosis skills.

**Q: What advice would you give a student starting rotations unsure of their specialty?**

"Always be open minded. Don't give up on a specialty based on one bad day or one preceptor." Despite expecting to love surgery, he appreciated every rotation and built meaningful relationships with residents.

**Q: How did you approach asking for feedback?**

He stressed the importance of vocal advocacy for feedback, noting that "Most residents and attendings try to avoid this." Students should request private meetings to demonstrate genuine educational investment.

**Q: What were your favorite rotations?**

Interpersonal dynamics primarily determined his preferences. He enjoyed working with friendly staff and preferred operating room experiences, making Surgery and Neurology his favorites.

**Q: How did you balance clinical responsibilities with studying?**

He recommended completing UWorld for each rotation and watching OME early. Despite reduced study capacity compared to preclinical years, he targeted one hour nightly and left when clinical duties concluded.

**Q: Did your career goals change during third year?**

No change occurred. His neurosurgery rotation at year's end solidified his surgical aspirations.

**Q: What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?**

He gained appreciation for medicine's transformative potential, recognizing the deep patient trust in physicians and how surgery "elucidated this the most."
