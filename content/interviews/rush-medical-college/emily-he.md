# Emily He, MS1 — Rush Medical College

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

Emily described her schedule as varying by day, with an "8am quiz from the self-study material" followed by class from 8:30am–12pm. Rush uses "a flipped classroom model in which we no longer have lectures for class." Self-study packets feature "a video presentation of an actor with a certain chief complaint, the patient chart" plus material covering "microbiology and immunology...pharmacology...pathology and histology." Classes meet "twice a week for the hard science material and once a week for clinical skills" with groups of "16–17 students" subdivided into "4 or 5 students on interactive case-based activities."

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

"Medical school has flipped classroom and no lectures, the style is very different from college." Her advice: "Don't be afraid to change up your study habits."

**Q: What did you appreciate about the curriculum?**

She acknowledged initial overwhelm with "a huge breadth of information from every discipline" but appreciated gaining "an understanding of diseases from the micro level to the macro level."

**Q: What are the most important things to learn about a medical school when deciding fit?**

She emphasized "know what you are looking for" and prioritized finding "an institution that was supportive and aligned with my passions." She valued experiencing "a sense of warmth from the faculty and students."

**Q: Can you describe Chicago and what you do for fun?**

Emily praised "many choices of coffee shop options" and "unlimited public transportation." Regarding safety concerns: "Rush is not in those areas or the places in Downtown that are close to the school."

**Q: What organizations are you involved in?**

Research, free clinic volunteer work, suture and intubation team, student professional interest groups, Asian American student organizations. She highlighted Rush's "overwhelming commitment to the community," noting initiatives like packaging "extra food from the hospital and cafeteria kitchens" for "the Franciscan Homeless Shelter."

**Q: How have you developed clinical skills?**

"We started hands-on patient care during our 3rd week of school." Her placements included "primary care, urogynecology" and orthopedic oncology.

**Q: What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?**

She learned that "medicine is becoming more and more integrated. No single healthcare discipline can do it on their own." Rush offered "Interprofessional Education (IPE)" with students from "nursing, medical lab science, speech pathology, audiology, health administration, cardio perfusion, OT, PT."

**Q: What resources have been most useful to you?**

Beyond "Sketchy and Pathoma," Emily found "my classmates were the most useful." The class shares materials via "a Google drive and GroupMe message group."

**Q: If a young undergraduate came to you asking about the best major, what would you say?**

"If you are interested in science, pursue a major that fulfills most of the pre-med requirements for medical school. Then for a second major, minor, or masters, you can explore your passion."

**Q: What is your favorite school event?**

"My favorite event has been the Chicago Bulls games. Rush are the official healthcare team for the Chicago Bulls and the Chicago White Sox."

**Q: What do you wish you had known as an undergraduate?**

"I wish I knew that everything would work out. I had a lot of doubt and always worried if I wasn't doing enough."
