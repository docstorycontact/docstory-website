# Allison Martin, MS2 — Chicago Medical School at Rosalind Franklin University

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

Allison's schedule varies significantly. She's a night owl who doesn't attend lecture. She typically wakes by 10AM, spends time organizing, attends mandatory afternoon labs, then studies from 7-8PM onward. She watches educational videos at double speed until midnight or 1AM, with 1-2 hours wind-down before sleeping around 2-3AM.

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

Lecturer quality is "variable from class to class" but generally superior to undergraduate standards. Medical school covers "a college semester's worth of material every 6 weeks," requiring significant adaptation regardless of lecturer quality.

**Q: How has your approach to learning/studying changed since undergrad?**

Her method transformed dramatically. Previously attending lectures with detailed notes, she now learns "by listening/reading" and spends considerably less time per unit of material. She emphasized that "everyone's different" and finding "how you personally learn most efficiently" matters most.

**Q: How would you describe the student culture at your school?**

Students demonstrate strong involvement in "student council or clubs or volunteering or research." The environment features mutual support: "we will show up to help support them." She noted the "supportive, collaborative atmosphere" influenced her school selection.

**Q: Can you describe the area surrounding your school?**

First-year students study in North Chicago, described as "a quiet suburb" with "restaurants, gyms, shopping." Upper-year students relocate to Chicago itself, offering greater urban amenities and opportunities.

**Q: What would you consider strengths of your medical school?**

Key strengths include caring faculty and responsive administration. Regarding clinical training, lacking hospital affiliation is actually advantageous, allowing rotations "through most of the hospitals in Chicago-land plus many small clinics," exposing students to diverse practice styles and patient populations.

**Q: What resources have been most useful for self-learning?**

"Pathoma, Sketchy, and talking it out with classmates or professors/physicians."

**Q: How have you developed clinical skills?**

Development occurred "not as much as I would like," though second-year included a preceptorship. Her class council actively works "to get more simulation added to the first 2 years."

**Q: What is one thing you would do differently?**

She would have "applied differently in my first application cycle. Otherwise, nothing."

**Q: How has self-doubt affected you?**

She emphasized three strategies: utilizing school counseling services, maintaining outside interests to maintain perspective, and recognizing that "med school isn't the only thing happening in the world." She stressed that external interests combat imposter syndrome through positive external feedback, concluding: "You have to make time for things outside of med school because that is what will keep you sane and grounded."
