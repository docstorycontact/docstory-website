# Drew Weinstein, MS2 — Chicago Medical School at Rosalind Franklin University

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

"Wake up at 6:30 AM. Get to the library at 7:30 AM and do some pre-viewing for the lectures to come and planning out my day. Go to class at 8:00-Noon. Lunch 12:00-1:00 PM. More lecture, anatomy class, or random mandatory class 1:00-3:30 PM. Study 3:30-9 or later depending how close it is to an exam. The latest is midnight. Gym either in the morning if I don't have lecture or during lunch/early afternoon."

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

"The lectures are moving way faster and often require more details (i.e. you spend 10 minutes on glycolysis but you focus on the enzymes and the limiting steps rather than the molecule structure.) A lot more clinical information is added. Almost all lectures are powerpoint. Instructors are either MD or PhD and most don't accept questions during class but after you can walk up to them and ask. Most don't have office hours but you can go to their office whenever. They all want you to succeed. They are not looking to fail anyone."

**Q: How has your approach to learning/studying changed since undergrad?**

"I use Anki and study every day. You cannot cram. I also utilize group study time."

**Q: What are the most important things to learn about a medical school when deciding fit?**

"Apply early and broadly. Reach and safety. I have friends with okay academics that applied to a ton of schools and got one interview at UCSF and got in. I have other friends with 15 interviews and no acceptances. Then once you get acceptances, look at schools where the students are happy and the school listens to their ideas for change. Also look at the match list and hospitals associated with their rotations. Work on your interviewing skills! You might think you're an expert but I can guarantee you are far from it."

**Q: Can you describe the area surrounding your school?**

"My school is in the middle of nowhere which is no fun but it is great to study. On the weekends we can take a train 45 minutes into the city to enjoy the nightlife or just to explore. During rotations third and fourth year we move downtown which is awesome."

**Q: In retrospect, which classes in undergrad were most useful?**

"Physiology, biochemistry, and cell biology."

**Q: What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?**

"You know nothing and everything you learn is not even a drop in the bucket!"

**Q: If a young undergraduate came to you asking about the best major, what would you say?**

"Do a major you will get the best GPA in. If that is Psych or something else, just do a post-bacc with a linkage after."

**Q: What do you wish you had known as an undergraduate?**

"How much your GPA matters and how it is very important to talk to your teachers since you will need letters of rec."

**Q: What is one thing you would do differently?**

"I used my time between undergrad and medical school wisely — working at a startup, research lab, scribing, medical assistant, volunteering for the underserved. But during undergrad, there will always be another party and it might be beneficial to study more the night before for that physics final."
