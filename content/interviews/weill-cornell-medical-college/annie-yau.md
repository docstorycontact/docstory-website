# Annie Yau, MS1 — Weill Cornell Medical College

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

"We start the day at 8AM either PBL, small group discussions, or lecture, and run through 1PM. Our afternoons are anatomy labs during the first semester, preceptorship, electives, or free time to study and nap."

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

She notes the material is consistently interesting and engaging, unlike some undergraduate courses. All students learn the same material, which can feel heavy, but she appreciates the consistent quality.

**Q: How has your approach to learning/studying changed since undergrad?**

"I have to be a lot more consistent with my studying just to keep up with the amount of material and the fast pace."

**Q: How would you describe the unique culture at your school?**

With just over 100 students living together first year, the class is tight-knit. "The culture here is pretty low-key, probably because we're all distracted from the stresses of medical school by being in New York."

**Q: Can you describe the area and things you do for fun?**

New York City offers museums, Central Park, theater discounts, and diverse food options. Her main adjustment involves winter weather as a California native.

**Q: What resources have been most useful for self-learning?**

"I use Pathoma and First Aid to supplement the lectures and make sure I know the important take home points."

**Q: What topics did interviewers focus on?**

Interviewers emphasized Cornell's relationships with Memorial Sloan Kettering, Rockefeller University, and Hospital for Special Surgery, highlighting research and clinical opportunities.

**Q: What would you do differently in undergrad?**

"I would have bought more season tickets to Cal basketball games."

**Q: How have doubts affected you?**

She emphasizes having strong support and avoiding comparison with others as essential for resilience.

**Q: How do you maintain mental health?**

Through support systems, meditation, and exercise. She credits the pass/fail curriculum as particularly helpful.
