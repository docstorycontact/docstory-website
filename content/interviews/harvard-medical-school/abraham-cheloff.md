# Abraham Cheloff, MS2 — Harvard Medical School

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

Cheloff explained he's in his core clerkship year following a 14-month preclinical curriculum. On inpatient weeks, he arrives around 6am to "pre round on my patients before rounding, which can last until noon." Afternoons involve "writing notes, calling consults, and coordinating with the rest of the care team." Outpatient clinics begin at 8:30-9am with chart review and patient encounters. During preclinical year, the school uses a flipped classroom with Case-Based Collaborative Learning structure, with classroom sessions Monday, Tuesday, Thursday, and Friday from 8am-12:30pm, afternoons free for preparation, and Wednesdays at clinical sites.

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

HMS's preclinical curriculum uses a distinctive flipped classroom model. Students prepare the night before using videos and annotated handouts, then "cases with both basic science and clinical questions are worked through in groups of 4 before being further discussed in the big group." Faculty facilitate discussions to ensure teaching points are covered.

**Q: How has your approach to learning and/or studying changed since you were an undergrad?**

Cheloff noted his study habits "hasn't changed as much as I expected." He's always been an active participant who reviews notes after class. The main shift during clinical year involves "becoming more efficient in my studying, as there is limited time outside of clinical duties." He found "limiting distractions like facebook has been most helpful."

**Q: How would you describe the student culture at your school?**

He credited HMS's pass/fail curriculum and lack of ranking, creating "a collaborative learning environment that allows friendships to blossom outside of the classroom." Notable events include a class music video and "FABRIC, a yearly multicultural class-show put on for Revisit weekend," where "the entire class comes together" on creative projects.

**Q: What would you consider strengths of your medical school?**

Cheloff highlighted "the passion of the educators here, both preclinical and clinical." Faculty provided "extra support, mentorship, or research and other extracurricular opportunities." He noted "the number of possibilities at HMS truly feels endless."

**Q: How much/well have you been able to develop clinical skills alongside your classroom work?**

During first year, "each Wednesday was dedicated to clinical skills" including longitudinal primary care clinic work, practicing history and physical exams on inpatients, and "round-robins where patients with specific conditions come and allow us to examine them." Entering his clinical year, he "felt extremely prepared clinically."

**Q: How has imposter syndrome affected you?**

Cheloff acknowledged imposter syndrome is "extremely pervasive in medical school given the number of amazing and intelligent colleagues and faculty." However, he noted that "for each instance in which someone else has an answer, there are going to be just as many times when you will be the one in a place to help others." Support from "an amazing partner and friends who lift me up" helped him persevere.

**Q: How do you maintain your mental health?**

The flexible schedule—classes 8am-12:30pm four days weekly—allowed time for "appointments and other commitments that need to occur during business hours." During clinical rotations, "dedicated 'flex' time" provides "a certain number of afternoons off" for necessary tasks.
