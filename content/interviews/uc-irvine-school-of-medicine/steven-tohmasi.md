# Steven Tohmasi, MS2 — UC Irvine School of Medicine

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

"During first year, we usually have our basic science classes from 9-12 with afternoon classes on occasions. We also have mandatory small group sessions (either clinical foundations, ultrasound, or simulation) once a week for 3 hours."

The school uses recorded lectures, allowing flexible attendance. Small group sessions teach clinical skills directly from preceptors.

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

The structure resembles undergrad — lectures from PhD or MD instructors with slides and textbooks. The key difference: "the volume of material you are responsible for knowing. You will often hear the analogy of med school being compared to drinking out of a fire hose."

**Q: What factors matter most when choosing a medical school?**

Steven recommends considering: tuition/cost of living, location, interview day impression, whether first and second years are pass/fail, recorded lecture access, and available student organizations, research, or community programs.

**Q: Describe your school's location and city.**

"Located in sunny Southern California, Orange County offers endless opportunities for fun." The campus sits less than 15 minutes from the ocean, with nearby hiking trails at Crystal Cove and restaurants. Los Angeles, San Diego, and Big Bear are accessible for weekend trips.

**Q: What organizations are you involved in?**

Steven highlights UCI's ultrasound education focus. "Each year, around 30-40 med students will spend the summer between their 1st and 2nd year teaching ultrasound internationally." He spent Summer 2018 in Switzerland teaching ultrasound at the University of Basel.

**Q: How have you developed clinical skills?**

Through the PACE program, a longitudinal clinical experience where students "spend one half day a week seeing real patients with a preceptor in clinic" during first and second year.

**Q: Advice on choosing an undergraduate major?**

"Choose the major that interests you the most. We tend to perform at our best when we are fascinated by the material we are learning. At the end of the day, admissions committees don't really care about what you major in as long as you perform well in your classes."

**Q: What do you wish you'd known as an undergraduate?**

Steven cautions against believing "it takes a spotless GPA and perfect MCAT to even get one's foot in the door." Most schools use holistic review, so "make it a priority to create the perfect balance between school and those things in life that you're truly passionate about."
