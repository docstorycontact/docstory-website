# Eric Nguyen, MS2 — Creighton University School of Medicine

**Q: What does your typical day of medical school look like?**

As an MS1, Eric woke at 7 AM, exercised, and streamed lectures starting at 9 AM until 1-2 PM. After lunch and a break, he studied from 5-6 PM through 10-11 PM, reviewing the day's lectures. He concluded evenings relaxing with reading, social media, or television shows.

**Q: How do your classes and lectures compare to those at your undergraduate institution?**

Medical school resembles undergraduate coursework in biochemistry, cell biology, genetics, and immunology, but emphasizes clinical applications rather than molecular theory. Instructors demonstrate greater devotion to student success, readily scheduling office hours and responding quickly to emails.

**Q: How has your approach to learning and/or studying changed since you were an undergrad?**

Eric adopted "active learning" through self-quizzing and flashcard creation rather than passive note-taking. He employs "spaced repetition," revisiting material multiple times across days rather than marathon study sessions. He takes frequent breaks following a 50-minutes-on, 10-minutes-off schedule.

**Q: What are the most important things to learn about a medical school when deciding fit?**

Three factors matter most: school culture and compatibility with personal temperament; location matching lifestyle preferences; and institutional alignment with career aspirations in medicine.

**Q: Can you describe Omaha and what you do for fun?**

Omaha impressed Eric with its welcoming residents and diverse food options. However, he missed California's natural features — hiking opportunities and year-round greenery. Travel to major cities required 7+ hour drives, though closer destinations like Kansas City remained accessible.

**Q: What organizations or activities are you involved in?**

Eric participates in the American Medical Association, co-founded the MICE Club (Medical Innovation Collaboration and Entrepreneurship Club), volunteers at free clinics, mentors schoolchildren, and serves homeless populations. He previously participated in Creighton's "Wisdom Groups," Jesuit-led discussions exploring meaning in medical practice.

**Q: In retrospect, which classes in undergrad were most useful?**

Ranked by importance: Biochemistry, Cell Biology, Immunology, Developmental Biology, and Genetics.

**Q: What is a unique aspect of education at your medical school?**

Creighton partnered with Firecracker for the Neuroscience course, supplementing lectures with flashcards and practice questions aligned with board exams (NBME and USMLE).

**Q: What is one way your outlook on medicine has changed?**

Eric deepened his understanding of medicine's humanistic dimensions. Beyond understanding bodily dysfunction, he learned to empathize with patients' daily struggles — how illness affects hobbies, family time, and financial stability.

**Q: If a young undergraduate came to you asking about the best major, what would you say?**

Pursue subjects of genuine interest while completing prerequisites. Future medicine involves collaboration across fields like biomedical engineering, business, computer science, and music. Well-rounded expertise enables innovative contributions.

**Q: How has self-doubt affected you?**

Eric maintains inner optimism that circumstances resolve positively. Exercise, meditation, and social connection sustain him. He avoids complaining, remembering his privileged position — "being healthy, alive, and with everything I need to live already puts me in the top 5-10% of the world."

**Q: What do you wish you had known as an undergraduate?**

Eric advises living authentically rather than for others' expectations. He emphasizes enjoying the process, noting "Your time in undergrad will absolutely fly by."

**Q: What is one thing you would do differently?**

He would have traveled and studied abroad more frequently. Travel broadens perspectives and cultural understanding. Summer breaks should involve international mission trips to develop appreciation for global disparities.
